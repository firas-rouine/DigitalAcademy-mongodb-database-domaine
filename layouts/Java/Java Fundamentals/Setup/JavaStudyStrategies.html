    Java Study Strategies

    Learning Objectives:

    • Provide study and tool tips
    • Emphasize the importance of solving problems for learning

    Fail Fast, Fail Early and Fail OFTEN

    Truly, being a self-directed learner is a skill that takes a lifetime to master. Work everyday to be curious, be humble, fail fast, fail early and fail often.

    Fail Fast

    DON'T spend too long on one problem without reaching out to other resources.

    Fail Early

    DON'T wait too long to test your code, delaying experience with extremely valuable errors.

    Fail Often

    If you don't take risks, or even experiment with trying to find possible ways to make errors, you won't be able to solve them as fast without the practice, and you'll miss out on actual knowledge gained.

    An expert is a person who has made all the mistakes that can be made in a very narrow field.
    ~ Niels Bohr

    To learn well and efficiently you have to actually experience all the setbacks. While it takes time and patience, the most critical step in learning is running into hurdles and working your way through them, but not staying stuck. Remember! No less than 20 minutes but no more. In fact, struggling and working through issues over and over (yes, the same issues) is the primary way we learn, and the most important part of the process, even as a seasoned developer. So again, and it bears repeating, fail fast, fail early and fail often!

    Importance of End-of-Day Reading

    No matter where you get to in the assignments by the end of the day, set aside time to do the reading for the next day, even if you are behind. It's also good to use as a time to reset, reflect and regroup. You will get much more from the morning sessions if you have some background knowledge going in so you can ask questions, reinforce your comprehension and participate. This is the backbone of a flipped classroom, and it's backed by learning science.


    Reading Documentation & Exploring the Language

    Java is a very deep well of a subject and can be very intimidating. Being a Java developer means being a life-long learner. Take the time now to start diving in, and going deeper on your own by:

    • Referencing the official documentation. For example, whenever a java.util class behavior or Spring JPA usage is unclear. Usually, you can google for official documentation fairly quickly, but we also provide quick-reference links in most modules.
    • Bookmarking other resources as a go-to for further questions. We all have found results on forums like StackOverflow, but other resources, specifically for Java references are out there:
      • javatpoint (a comprehensive Java reference site)
      • Baeldung (for the Spring section of the course.)
    • Explore Spring Tool Suite! Once we switch from Visual Studio to the Spring Tool Suite IDE in the OOP Sections, you will find it has menu pop ups and explanations for all things Java and Spring. Don't ignore those. You can learn a TON just from your IDE.
