    How to Problem-Solve

    Whenever you're given an assignment or a problem to solve here in the bootcamp, you'll likely want to try and jump straight to "how would I do this in code?" question. That's kind of like if someone told you "we need a dessert for this birthday party" you jumping to "how many eggs do I need?" without first figuring out what dessert you even want to make. 

    It's a normal desire: to use the skills your learning about coding to tackle the issue right in front of you. But here's the important part: tech is always only one way to solve a problem. So, start by understanding the problem, not the method of solution. 

    Below is a tried and true problem-solving framework  that should take less than 5 mins as prep to set yourself up for success when trying to problem solve. (Credit for framework: Assistant Professor of Teaching and Learning at University of Kansas, Kelli Thomson)

    T.H.I.N.K.

    TALK about the problem to others or your rubber duck. What's the situation? What is the problem asking for? What information is provided? 

    HOW can the problem be solved? DO NOT THINK ABOUT CODE HERE. This is where you tinker with ideas of how the information relates to each other. We recommend pen to paper, draw ideas out, make a picture. Do what works for you. 

    IDENTIFY a strategy for solving the problem and try to apply it. DO NOT CODE YET. This should focus on finding as many ways the information can interact with each other to get the results you need. Don't stop with just one strategy. See if you can always get two strategies. 

    NOTICE how your strategy is working or not. Do you need to adjust your strategy? Your approach? This is where you pseudo code.

    KEEP thinking about the problem. Are you actually solving the problem or are you caught in the weeds of the solution? 

    EXAMPLE

    Problem: Sort the following numbers from least to greatest: 1, 5, 341, 2, 46 
    TALK: "So, I'm given a list of numbers and need to organize them based on numeric value. Cool."

    HOW: "Well, we organize numbers based on whether they are higher or lower than other numbers in the group. So, I need a solution that can do that."

    IDENTIFY a strategy: I guess I could try it by comparing each number to each other one and have it stop when it reaches a spot when they are less than the number to the right but more than the number to the right.  Like, is 1 higher than 5 (no, so it goes to the left of the 5), then is 5 higher than 1 (yes), is 5 higher than 341, (no, so it goes to the left of 341 but right of the 1).  341 higher than 1 (yes), is it higher than 5 (yes), is it higher than 2 (yes), is it higher than 46 (yes, so it goes to the right of 46). Is 2 higher than 1 (yes), higher than 5 (no, so it stops just before 5), etc. 

    NOTICE: On paper, it looks good. let me pseudo code it out: 

    //How do I hold all the numbers together? I can use an array. Ooh! I can use index numbers to help with this. 

    //Take the number at an index point and compare it to the other spots in the index to see if it's higher or lower. Once it finds a spot in the index where it's greater to the one of the left and less than the one on the right, it stops.

    //Pretty sure in order to do this I'm gonna need arrays, loops, and conditions. Let's take it one thing at a time. Array first...

    KEEP: Does the strategy actually get the numbers to sort from least to greatest?   


    TIP: YOU DON"T HAVE TO HAVE A PERFECT SOLUTION TO BEGIN WITH. As you work through the problem, you can change and update and grow.
