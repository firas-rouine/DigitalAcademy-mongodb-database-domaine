    You Will Get Stuck: What to Do

    When you run into issues - which all developers do - don't fret. Remember that you're learning how to ask questions, explore to find answers, and become a self-sufficient developer. So, here's what you should do when you get stuck:


    Step 1: Talk to the Rubber Duck

    Step 2: The 20 Minute Rule

    Step 1: Talk to the Rubber Duck

    When developers run into issues, a common practice is to walk someone else through the code, line by line. They start by explaining the following:

    • What was I trying to get this to do? (What was I expecting it to do?)
    • Where in the code are you trying to do this?
    • What does each line do? 

    Developers recognized early on that when they walked someone else through their code, they often found the error or found a solution to the problem. This makes sense: walking another person through your code is critical to learning and understanding what you need to do next. So, developers will often grab a rubber ducky and walk it through their code. Seriously, they do: 

    Don't believe us? There's an entire website dedicated to it.

    But Why? 

    The idea first came to the masses in the book The Pragmatic Programmer where they explain that rubber duck debugging allowed developers to improve their practice and solve their problems on their own. Developers walk their rubber ducks through the code because in the workplace, time is money, and so grabbing another developer to sit and listen to you explain your code only to have you find the answer yourself means that two developers were on a problem but only one was needed to solve it. 

    So go get yourself a rubber ducky. Or borrow one of your kid's stuffed animals. 

    If walking your duck through the code doesn't help, move on to Step 2. 


    Step 2: The 20-Minute Rule

    At Digital Academy, we created a problem-solving framework to help you and your cohort mates strengthen two of the most sought after skills of developers: resourcefulness and self-sufficient developers. We call it The 20-Minute Rule. The 20-Minute Rule helps you build the know-how and discipline you need to problem-solve on your own initiative, and once you have spent the time trying different methods to solve the problem, then ask your peers for advice. If you're still stuck, you try a different avenue. At the final point, the instructors can help you out of a pickle. This is a critical part of Digital Academy and what makes our program so effective. Embrace it and in due time, you will realize its potential.

    How

    When you run into an issue, and the rubber ducky debugging hasn't helped, then do the following.

    1. Ask yourself: What questions do I need answered before I can move forward?
    2. Explore and Tinker for 20 mins Solo: Explore the Digital Academy content to review a concept or two that might help me find those answers. Explore the documentation online, use your Google and critical analysis skills to help you.
    3. Explore and Tinker for 20 mins with a Buddy: Talk with a cohort-mate or two to see if they can help you find answers together.
    4. Lastly, reach out to your TA or an instructor to see if they can help you understand where your approach to solving this problem broke down and guide you in the right direction.

    When you approach an instructor, make sure you can answer:

    • What was I trying to get this to do? (What was I expecting it to do?)
    • Where in the code are you trying to do this?
    • What actually happened?
    • What have I done to try to fix it? 

    We are happy to help you, and we will gladly help you learn how to find the answers you need. This might feel frustrating at first; most traditional classroom education is modeled where the instructor provides the answers. However, as novice developers, your greatest skill will be to learn what questions you should be asking.

    The 20-Minute Rule actually saves you time

    The 20-minute rule may sound like we’re asking you to put a lot of time into solving your own problems, but it’s actually a tool to keep you moving forward. We’ve noticed that programmers can often be very stubborn! Sometimes students keep working on a bug for an hour or two or six. We ask you to keep your struggle down to 20 minutes. 20 minutes is enough so that you receive the maximum value from practicing your debugging skills. After 20 minutes, it is time to put a cap on your struggle so that you can proceed with your learning. We have too much material to let everyone take all the time they need to solve all their problems on their own. After 20 minutes of struggle, be humble enough to ask for help, and this way you’ll receive the benefit of the struggle without falling behind our rigorous schedule.

    After the struggle

    The 20-minute rule is designed so that you will solve your own problems to the best of your ability and become self-sufficient. What this means is that you may be left wondering, “Did I solve this correctly? Did I use best practices?” Try not to worry too much about this when you are learning a new concept. It’s a good idea to compare your code with your cohort mates to see different approaches to the same problem, but right now you should not be concerned with having the best code. The important thing is that you made it work and you understand how it works. Keep moving forward, and you will have more opportunities in the future to write more refined code. In a few months, you will be able to look back at your earlier code, and you will enjoy seeing how much you’ve grown. We have a lot of material to cover, so don’t let yourself fall behind in pursuit of perfection.

    Falling behind

    Even with the 20-minute rule, it is normal to fall behind schedule. Life happens. We catch a cold or suffer a migraine. Family obligations pop up. We have to travel, or sometimes, we just don’t keep up with our assignments. When this happens, do not hesitate to ask your instructor for advice! Your instructor will more than likely find a way to help you get caught up or otherwise customize a plan that better suits you.
